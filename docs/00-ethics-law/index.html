Why this comes first
Same skills. Different ethics.

Most "learn hacking" resources hand you a port scanner on page one. That is how beginners end up in trouble without realising they did anything wrong. The techniques are genuinely useful and genuinely legal — when you have authorisation. Without it, the exact same command is a criminal offence.

By the end of this module you will be able to: explain what makes security testing legal versus illegal; describe the UK Computer Misuse Act 1990; write a scope and Rules of Engagement document; follow responsible disclosure when you find a real vulnerability; and recognise the legal outlets for your skills.

Section 1
The one idea that matters: authorisation
⚖

Permission is the line between a penetration tester and a criminal.

A penetration tester runs a port scan against a client's server and gets paid. A stranger runs the identical scan against that same server and commits an offence. The packets are the same. The law cares about one thing the packets don't show: did the owner say yes, in writing, in advance?

Three questions to ask yourself before any action against any system:

Q1
Do I own this system, or have explicit written permission to test it?If the answer is no — stop. There is no "I was only looking" defence.
Q2
Is what I'm about to do inside the agreed scope?If it isn't written in the scope document, you are not authorised to touch it.
Q3
Could this cause harm or disruption I haven't been authorised to cause?"It was right next to something in scope" is not authorisation.
Section 2
The law — UK Computer Misuse Act 1990

The CMA is the core UK law governing computer access. You are responsible for knowing it.

SectionOffencePlain English
s.1Unauthorised access to computer materialGetting into a system you weren't allowed into — even just looking, even if nothing is damaged.
s.2Unauthorised access with intent to commit further offencesAs above, but planning to do something else with that access.
s.3Unauthorised acts impairing operationDamaging, disrupting, or degrading a system — e.g. deleting data, denial of service.
s.3AMaking, supplying or obtaining tools for the aboveCreating or distributing hacking tools intended for criminal offences.
⚠

Common mistakes beginners make:

• Curiosity is not a defence. "I just wanted to see if it worked" does not make unauthorised access legal.

• Lack of damage is not a defence. Under s.1, merely accessing without permission is already an offence.

• A site being insecure is not an invitation. An unlocked door is still not yours to walk through.

• Testing a friend's site "to help" without written permission is still unauthorised.

ℹ

Other laws that interact with security work: the Data Protection Act 2018 / UK GDPR (if you encounter personal data), the Investigatory Powers Act, and equivalent laws in other jurisdictions. If a target system is in another country, that country's law may also apply to you.

Section 3
Scope & Rules of Engagement

Professionals never test on a handshake. Every engagement requires two short documents, written and signed before any testing begins.

Scope — WHAT and WHERE

  • Which systems, IP ranges, domains are in scope?
  • What is explicitly out of scope?
  • What time windows is testing permitted in?
  • Who is authorised to grant permission?

Rules of Engagement — HOW

  • Which techniques are permitted / forbidden?
  • Emergency contacts if something breaks
  • How sensitive data is handled and destroyed
  • Reporting format and deadline
scope_statement_template.txt
# SCOPE STATEMENT — [Client Name]
In scope : [domain/IP/application]
Out of scope : [email servers, staff, third-party services, all other domains]
Window : [permitted testing times]
Authorised by : [Name, Role — Date]
# RULES OF ENGAGEMENT
Permitted : [web app testing, automated scanning of in-scope host]
Forbidden : [DoS, social engineering, access to real customer data]
Emergency : [Name / Phone]
Data handling : findings encrypted; destroyed 30 days post-report
Deliverable : written report within [X] working days
Section 4
Responsible Disclosure

If you find a genuine vulnerability — during a bug bounty or by accident — responsible disclosure is how you act ethically and stay on the right side of the law.

01
Stop. Do not explore further than needed to confirm the issue. Do not access or download other people's data.
02
Document what you found, minimally and factually — screenshots, request/response pairs, impact description.
03
Report privately to the owner via their security@ address, bug bounty platform, or published vulnerability-disclosure policy.
04
Give them time to fix it before telling anyone else. Industry norm is around 90 days — follow the programme's policy.
05
Never extort. "Pay me or I'll publish" turns responsible disclosure into a criminal offence.

Write Your First Scope & RoE

Lab Scenario

A fictional client, Acme Bakery Ltd, asks you to test their website shop.acme-bakery.test — a domain they own. They do not want their email server, staff, or any other system touched.

Your deliverable — Scope Statement

  • In scope: shop.acme-bakery.test web application only
  • Out of scope: mail servers, staff, physical premises, all other domains
  • Testing window: weekdays 18:00–22:00 (client local time)
  • Authorised by: [name / role / date]

Your deliverable — Rules of Engagement

  • Permitted: web application testing, automated scanning of in-scope host only
  • Forbidden: denial of service, social engineering, accessing real customer data
  • Emergency contact: [name / phone]
  • Data handling: findings stored encrypted, destroyed 30 days after report
  • Deliverable: written report within 7 working days
